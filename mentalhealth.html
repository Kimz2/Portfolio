Overview


Mentalhealth.gov provides information and support to Americans on the topic of mental health. Our primary goal with the redesign was to convey the philosophy that Mental Health Matters. We wanted to deviate from the traditional look of government websites by creating a welcoming atmosphere that evokes lightness and simplicity.

Problem

Mentalhealth.gov fails to stand out as a resource for Americans looking to educate themselves on the topic of mental health. Due to its lack of direction and outdated UI, it seems to get lost among other resources provided by the government. Appearing more as an afterthought than a credible source of knowledge and support.

Solution

Our two main points of focus were the UI and the sitemap. We wanted to improve the UI by designing an encouraging environment where users can inform themselves and have easy access to resources that offer help.

Architecture


Architecture

The sitemap was the primary obstacle for our users. The navigation menu had obscure categories like "basics," failing to provide direction. Disorders were listed under "What to Look for," which was an additional source of confusion.

My goal was to declutter and simplify the navigation menu. I combined related pages, chose straightforward titles, and added a page for the LGBTQ+ community, who are 3 times more likely to experience a mental health condition.

Iterations


Iterations


Iterations


Iterations


After creating the initial wireframes, Melissa and I were not happy with the outcome. We’d spent the majority of our time on research and revising the sitemap. With a week left until our deadline, we felt our layout looked generic and dull.


Melissa proposed we set aside our existing wireframe and start fresh on a blank page so we could experiment with new concepts without being tied down to our preset design ideas. Even though we worked separately, our aesthetics were alike. We both had utilized white space and drew inspiration from the Memphis design movement. Our brainstorming session allowed us to break out of the constraints set by our original wireframes and catapulted us into a new world of ideas.

Melissa expressed her concern with our symptom checker tool, suggesting that it might be used as a means for self-diagnosis. Even though our users wanted something to help narrow down the conditions, we were worried about the implications and decided not to include that feature.

Analyzing our first version of the logo, we noticed it looked more like a piece of toast than anything else.

Aesthetics


Aesthetics


Aesthetics


Aesthetics


Aesthetics


Stylistically, the website leans towards a gentle, friendly atmosphere. Inspired by the Memphis design movement, we used geometric shapes to help soften the setting and create a joyful and comforting experience. We avoided using imagery that construed somber undertones to avoid conjuring negative feelings in users.

Future Improvements

  • Enhance our design by adding more refinement and cohesion
  • Revise the mobile version
  • Focus on animating additional interactive details that are fast
    but delightful, giving users a more lively end-to-end experience

We would also recommend for the Department of HHS to focus on refreshing the content to be more solutions-focused and approachable for the average American. To demonstrate this, we've used some written excerpts from NAMI.org in our prototype.


We encountered some challenges during the design process, which ultimately helped us improve our technical abilities and our openness to iteration. Redesigning at the last minute was a risky venture, we weren't able to pay attention to every detail, and there are some inconsistencies in our design. Still, it was a pivotal point in our process, enabling us to articulate our vision fully.
